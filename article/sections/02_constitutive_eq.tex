

The governing equations presented in Section \ref{sec:nonlinear continuum electromechanics} are coupled by means of a suitable constitutive law. 
The objective of the following section is to introduce some notions on  constitutive laws in thermo-electro-viscoelasticity. 


\subsection{The free energy density function}

The system of PDEs presented in equations \eqref{eqn:local form conservation of linear momentum}, \eqref{eqn:Gauss law}, \eqref{eqn:Faraday} and \eqref{eqn:local form energy our format} is coupled in a nonlinear fashion through the constitutive response of the material. In the case of electro-thermo-viscoelasticity, the free energy density per unit of undeformed volume, denoted as $\Psi$, will determine the specific dependence of  $\{\vect{P},\vect{D}_0,\eta,\Dvis\}$  as functions of  $\{\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\}$, where $\A=\{\vect{A}_1,\vect{A}_2,\dots,\vect{A}_{n_\text{Maxw}}\}$ represent the set of internal variables $\vect{A}_{\alpha}$, ${\alpha} = \{1, . . . , n_\text{Maxw}\}$,
and $n_\text{Maxw}$ the number of Maxwell branches used to model the viscoelastic behaviour of the material. 
The Helmholtz free-energy density function $\Psi$, defined per unit
undeformed volume and characterising the constitutive response of the
elastic body $\mathcal{B}_0$, is assumed to depend on the deformation
gradient field $\vect{F}$, the material electric field $\vect{E}_0$,
the temperature field $\theta$, and a set of internal variables
$\A$ (assumed here to consist of $3\times3$ tensor-valued quantities),
namely
%
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\Psi:\mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}\times \mathbb{R}^{3}
		\times \mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3 \times n_\text{Maxw}}
		&\rightarrow \mathbb{R},\\
		\Psi &= \Psi\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right).
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
%

Using thermodynamical principles, the dissipation inequality $\Dvis$ in the context of thermo-electro-viscoelasticity can be written as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:disssipation1}
	\Dvis = \vect{P}:\dot{\vect{F}} - \vect{D}_0\cdot \dot{\vect{E}}_0 - \eta \dot{\theta} - \dot{\Psi}\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)\geq 0
\end{equation}
%
where the time derivative of the free energy density ${\Psi}\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)$ is computed as 
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:disssipation2}
	\dot{\Psi}\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)=\partial_{\vect{F}}\Psi:\dot{\vect{F}} +
	\partial_{\vect{E}_0}\Psi\cdot\dot{\vect{E}}_0 + 
	\partial_{\theta}\Psi \dot{\theta} + 
	\sum_{i={\alpha}}^{n_\text{Maxw}}\partial_{\vect{A}_{\alpha}}\Psi: \dot{\vect{A}_{\alpha}}
\end{equation}
%

Use of the Coleman and Noll procedure in \eqref{eqn:disssipation1} and \eqref{eqn:disssipation2} yields the following standard constitutive relationships 
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:constitutive 1}
	\begin{aligned}
		\vect{P}\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)=\left.\partial_{\vect{F}}\Psi\right\vert_{\mathcal{A}=\text{const}}; \quad 
		%
		\vect{D}_0\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)=\left.-\partial_{\vect{E}_0}\Psi\right\vert_{\mathcal{A}=\text{const}};	\quad
		%
		\eta\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)=\left.-\partial_{\theta}\Psi\right\vert_{\mathcal{A}=\text{const}};	
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
%
permitting to simplify the dissipation inequality as
%
\begin{equation}
	\Dvis\left(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\A\right)=-	\sum_{i={\alpha}}^{n_\text{Maxw}}\partial_{\vect{A}_{\alpha}}\Psi: \dot{\vect{A}_{\alpha}}
\end{equation}


In this section we will present the specific form of the energy density function encapsulating the constitutive coupled thermo-electro-mechanical response of the dielectric elastomer $\mathcal{B}_0$, emphasising on the mathematical/physical constraints that this need to comply with.

\subsection{Thermal variation and third law of thermodynamics}
\label{sec:third_law_thermodynamics}

The Third Law of thermodynamics establishes that \textit{“the entropy of a body should be zero at Kelvin state (zero temperature)”}, namely 
%
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{\theta \rightarrow 0} \eta = 0 \,.
\end{equation}



Accordingly, the free-energy density function must be formulated so as
to satisfy these thermodynamic requirements. In particular, the specific
heat coefficient, denoted by $c_v$ and defined as
%
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:specific_heat}
	c_v(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)
	=
	-\theta \frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial\theta^2}
	=
	\theta \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial\theta},
\end{equation}
%
must remain strictly positive ($c_v>0$). This requirement implies that
the Helmholtz free-energy density $\Psi$ must be concave with respect to
the temperature, namely
%
\[
\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial\theta^2}<0.
\]
%
Furthermore, at the reference configuration, the constitutive model must
recover the reference specific heat coefficient $c_v^0$.
To simultaneously guarantee these properties and ensure strict
compliance with the Third Law of Thermodynamics, we adopt the framework
recently proposed by Bonet and Gil
\cite{BonetGil2026}. Following a common assumption in the literature,
the thermal contribution is considered independent of the mechanical and
electrical effects, leading to the multiplicative decomposition
%
\begin{subequations} \label{eqn:multiplicative_cv}
	\begin{equation}
		c_v(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)
		=
		c_v^0 \X(\F,\E_0,\A) g(\theta),
		\qquad
		\X(\I,\0,\I)=1,
		\qquad
		g(\theta_R)=1.
		\tag{\ref*{eqn:multiplicative_cv}a,b,c}
	\end{equation}
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:multiplicative_cv_a}%
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:multiplicative_cv_b}%
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:multiplicative_cv_c}%
\end{subequations}
%
Here, $c_v^0$ denotes the reference specific heat coefficient,
$\X(\F,\E_0,\A)$ is a dimensionless function accounting for the
dependence of the specific heat on the mechanical and electrical
fields, and $g(\theta)$ is a purely thermal function describing the
temperature dependence.

Subsequently, a more general representation based on an additive
decomposition will be introduced in order to model different thermal
responses or thermal components, namely
%
\begin{subequations} \label{eqn:generalised_cv_additive}
	\begin{equation}
		c_v(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)
		=
		\sum_{i=1}^{n}
		c_{v,i}^0 \X_i(\F,\E_0,\A) g_i(\theta),
		\qquad
		\sum_{i=1}^{n}
		c_{v,i}^0
		\X_i(\I,\vect{0},\vect{I})
		=
		c_v^0.
		\tag{\ref*{eqn:generalised_cv_additive}a,b}
	\end{equation}
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:generalised_cv_additive_a}%
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:generalised_cv_additive_b}%
\end{subequations}
%

%If such generalization is considered, Eq. \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_cv_b} should be rewritten as \eqref{eqn:generalised_cv_additive_b}.

For the sake of clarity, we initially restrict attention to the case of
a single thermal component ($i=1$). Substitution of the multiplicative representation
\eqref{eqn:multiplicative_cv} into the definition of the specific heat
coefficient \eqref{eqn:specific_heat}, followed by integration with
respect to the temperature, leads to the following expression for the
entropy:
%%Integration of the specific heat coefficient in \eqref{eqn:specific_heat} with respect to temperature after having substituted the multiplicative decomposition in \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_cv} allows to obtain the following expression for the entropy:
\begin{subequations} \label{eqn:entropy_integral}
\begin{equation}
  \eta(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A) = \eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A) G(\theta) \,;\quad
  G(\theta) = 
    \frac{1}{\xi_R}\int_{0}^{\theta} \frac{g(\vartheta)}{\vartheta} \, d\vartheta 
    % \left(
    %   1 + \frac{1}{\xi_R} \int_{0}^{\theta} \frac{g(\vartheta)}{\vartheta} \, d\vartheta
    % \right) ,
  \tag{\ref*{eqn:entropy_integral}a,b}
\end{equation}
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:entropy_integral_a}%
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:entropy_integral_b}%
\end{subequations}
%
where we have introduced the entropy at the reference temperature $\eta_R(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\mathcal{A})$, which is defined as 
%
\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:entropy_definitions}
\begin{equation}
  \eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A) = c_v^0 \X(\F,\E_0,\A)\, \xi_R
    \quad \text{and} \qquad
    \xi_R = \int_0^{\theta_R} \frac{g(\vartheta)}{\vartheta} \, d\vartheta \,,
  \tag{\ref*{eqn:entropy_definitions}a,b}
\end{equation}
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:entropy_definitions_a}%
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:entropy_definitions_b}%
\end{subequations}
%
where the entropy $\eta_R$ at the reference configuration is a function that bypasses the definition of $\X$ and $\xi_R$ is a scaling constant relating the entropy with the specific heat.

%
\begin{equation*}
	c_v(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\mathcal{A})=\frac{\eta_R(\vect{F},\vect{E}_9,\mathcal{A})}{\xi_R}g(\theta)
\end{equation*}
%
which for the case of various contributions, the analogous to equation \eqref{eqn:generalised_cv_additive} would be
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:additive cv etaR}
	c_v(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\mathcal{A})=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\eta_{R,i}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_9,\mathcal{A})}{\xi_{R,i}}g_i(\theta)
\end{equation}

Analogously, further integration of \eqref{eqn:entropy_integral} with
respect to the temperature, recalling that
$\eta=-\partial_{\theta}\Psi$ (see \eqref{eqn:constitutive 1}),
yields the following expression for the free-energy density:
%
%
\begin{subequations} \label{eqn:free_energy_density_integral}
\begin{equation}
	\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A) = \Psi_K(\F,\E_0,\A) - \eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A)\,\zeta_R\,f(\theta) \,;\quad
  f(\theta) = \frac{1}{\zeta_R} \int_{0}^{\theta} G(\vartheta) \, d\vartheta \,,
  \tag{\ref*{eqn:free_energy_density_integral}a,b}
\end{equation}
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_a}%
\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_b}%
\end{subequations}
with
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:free_energy_density_definitions}
    \zeta_R = \int_{0}^{\theta_R} G(\vartheta) \,d\vartheta
      = \frac{1}{\xi_R}\int_{0}^{\theta_R} g(\vartheta) \left(
        \frac{\theta}{\vartheta} -1
      \right) \, d\vartheta \,.
\end{equation}

However, the parallelism between the expressions for the entropy and the free energy density is not complete, as the free energy density includes an additional term $\Psi_K(\F,\E_0,\A)$, which is a function of the mechanical and electrical physics but independent of the temperature. This term $\Psi_K$ can be interpreted as the Kelvin state or the free energy density at zero temperature. The choice of $\Psi_K$ will be discussed in more detail in the following section, where specific expressions for the different contributions to the free energy density will be presented. Another difference with the entropy is that the scaling parameter $\zeta_R$ has units of temperature, while $\xi_R$ is dimensionless.


Following \cite{BonetGil2026}, it is convenient to reformulate
\eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_a} in terms of the free-energy
density at the reference temperature,
$\Psi_R(\F,\E_0,\A)$, rather than in terms of the Kelvin-state
contribution. To this end, the following relation is introduced (see
\cite{BonetGil2026})
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:kelvin energy}
	\Psi_R(\F,\E_0,\A)
	=
	\Psi_K(\F,\E_0,\A)
	-
	\eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A)\zeta_R,
\end{equation}
%
which allows the free-energy density
$\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)$ to be expressed as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant}
	\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)
	=
	\Psi_R(\F,\E_0,\A)
	-
	\eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A)\,\zeta_R
	\bigl(f(\theta)-1\bigr).
\end{equation}
%%


An alternative representation of
\eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant} can be obtained in
terms of the internal-energy density at the reference temperature,
denoted by $\Eps_R(\F,\E_0,\A)$. This quantity is related to the
reference free-energy density through
%
\begin{equation*}\label{eqn:internal energy reference temperature}
	\Eps_R(\F,\E_0,\A)
	=
	\Psi_R(\F,\E_0,\A)
	+
	\eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A)\theta_R.
\end{equation*}
%
Substitution of
\eqref{eqn:internal energy reference temperature} into
\eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant} yields the following
equivalent expression for the free-energy density
$\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)$:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant 2}
	\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)
	=
	\Eps_R(\F,\E_0,\A)
	-
	\eta_R(\F,\E_0,\A)\,\zeta_R
	\left(
	f(\theta)-1+\frac{\theta_R}{\zeta_R}
	\right).
\end{equation}
%

Specific choices for the coupling functions
$\Eps_R$, $\eta_R$, and $f$ determine the constitutive response of the
different material contributions---namely, the viscoelastic and
electro-mechanical components---as well as their coupling with the
thermal field. The following section introduces admissible definitions
for the thermal function $f$, whereas
Section~\ref{sec:free_energy_density_terms} presents the constitutive
choices for $\Eps_R$ and $\eta_R$, and consequently for the reference
free-energy density $\Psi_R$.
%



\subsubsection{Thermal functions}


Recall from the previous section that, for the case of a single thermal
component, the thermo-mechanical coupling is governed by the function
$g(\theta)$ introduced in \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_cv}. 
The choice of $g(\theta)$ is, however, not arbitrary. It must satisfy
the mathematical and thermodynamic requirements of the constitutive
framework, including the positivity of the specific heat coefficient and
compliance with the Third Law of Thermodynamics. As an illustrative
example, assuming a constant specific heat coefficient, corresponding to
$g(\theta)=1$, would violate the Third Law. At the same time, the
adopted thermal functions must remain sufficiently flexible to represent
a broad range of thermal responses.

In practice, these thermal effects are more conveniently characterised
through the function $f(\theta)$, which is related to $g(\theta)$
through \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral}. Accordingly, three
different classes of functions $f(\theta)$ are introduced in the
following, each aimed at representing a distinct type of thermal
behaviour. The corresponding functions $g(\theta)$ are then obtained
through the inverse relation between both quantities.
%

%from which function $f(\theta)$ (see equation \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral}) can be obtained. 



\paragraph{Entropic elasticity}
Some classical models such as entropic elasticity or the thermoelasticity model for rubber in \cite{treloar1975} can be recovered by choosing the thermal functions as proposed by the following function $f(\theta)$ in \cite{BonetGil2026}
\begin{equation}    \label{eqn:thermal_function_f_bonet}
    f_A(\theta) 
    = \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\gamma+1};\qquad \gamma > 0
\end{equation}
%
from which its associated function $g(\theta)$ can be obtained as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:thermal_function_g_bonet}	
    g_A(\theta) = \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\gamma} . 
\end{equation}

In \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_bonet}, positivity of the parameter $\gamma$ is enforced to guarantee the positivity of the specific heat coefficient.


\paragraph{Nonlinear melting}
In \cite{BonetGil2026}, the authors propose a nonlinear melting model for which the function $f(\theta)$ should follow a dependence with respect to temperature of the form
%
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting}
  f_B(\theta) = \frac{1 - \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_M}\right)^{\gamma+1}}
                     {1 - \left(\frac{\theta_R}{\theta_M}\right)^{\gamma+1}}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_M$ is a parameter governing the melting temperature of the material, at which the free energy density vanishes. Integration of $f_B(\theta)$ according to \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral} permits to obtain $g_B(\theta)$ as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:thermal_function_g_melting}
g_B(\theta) = \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^\gamma
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Nonlinear softening}


In the context of the additive decomposition of the free-energy density
introduced in \eqref{eqn:generalised_cv_additive}, the calibration study
presented in Section~\ref{sec:calibration} revealed the need to
incorporate additional thermal behaviours for certain material
components, particularly nonlinear softening responses associated with a
free-energy contribution that is convex with respect to the
temperature. Although convexity of the free-energy density with respect
to temperature may appear to contradict the positivity of the specific
heat coefficient, thermodynamic admissibility must be assessed only
after assembling all contributions to the total free-energy density. The
resulting constraints associated with the specific constitutive choices
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:free_energy_density_terms} will therefore
be analysed in SECTION.

To describe this class of thermal response, we introduce the following scaling function $u(\theta)$ based on a  modified Weibull distribution of the form
%
\begin{equation}
  u(\theta) = \exp\left(
    - \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_T}\right)^{\gamma+1}
  \right) ; \quad \gamma > 0 \,,
\end{equation}
where $\theta_T$ is a parameter governing the transition temperature of the material properties. The function $f$ is then defined as a scaled and shifted version of $u$ as
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:thermal_function_f_softening}
  f_C(\theta) = \frac{(1-\delta)u(\theta) + \delta}{(1-\delta)u(\theta_R) + \delta} \,; \quad \delta \in [0,1) \,,
\end{equation}
%
which permits, after integration according to \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral}, to obtain $g_C(\theta)$ as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:thermal_function_g_softening}
	g_C(\theta) = \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\gamma}
  \frac{u(\theta)}{u(\theta_R)}
  \frac{
    \gamma -(\gamma+1)\left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_T}\right)^{\gamma+1}
  }{
    \gamma -(\gamma+1)\left(\frac{\theta_R}{\theta_T}\right)^{\gamma+1}
  }
\end{equation}



Figure \ref{fig:comparison_thermal_laws_f} shows a comparison of the different thermal functions $f_i$ presented in this section, which will be used in the later section \ref{sec:calibration} to capture different thermal responses of the material, showing the evolution of the energy density function $\Psi$ scaled with the energy density function $\Psi_R$ for increasing $\theta/\theta_R$ considering the other variables with fixed values of $\left\{\vect{F}=\vect{I},\vect{E}_0=\vect{0},\vect{A}_i=\vect{I}\right\}$. Specifically, from the definition of $\Psi$ in  \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant}, we fix the reference component $\Psi_R$ and also the reference entropy $\eta_R$, and study $\Psi/\Psi_R$ with respect to $\theta/\theta_R$. An important qualitative aspect associated with the nonlinear softening function is that in that case, the energy density function decays with increasing temperature but the ratio $\Psi/\Psi_R$ never becomes negative.

\subsection{Free energy density decomposition}
\label{sec:free_energy_density_terms}

In Eqs.~\eqref{eqn:generalised_cv_additive} and \eqref{eqn:additive cv etaR}, the specific heat coefficient $c_v$ was introduced through an additive decomposition into several contributions, without explicitly identifying their physical origin. In practice, these contributions emerge naturally from the different terms composing the strain energy density function $\Psi(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta,\mathcal{A})$. Accordingly, we adopt the following additive decomposition of the free energy density into  volumetric, long-term isochoric, non-equilibrium (viscoelastic) isochoric, and electro-mechanical contributions, denoted as $\{\bar\Psi,\hat\Psi_{\infty},\hat\Psi_{\alpha},\Psi_{\epsilon}\}$, respectively, namely
%
%On section \ref{sec:third_law_thermodynamics}, the thermal variation of the free energy density has been uncoupled from the mechanical and electrical physics via a multiplicative decomposition in Eq. \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_cv}. Considering the additive decomposition of the specific heat coefficient in Eq. \eqref{eqn:generalised_cv_additive} it can be can be propagated to the free energy density, namely, volumetric and isochoric contributions $\bar\Psi$ and $\hat\Psi$.
%Additional physics such as electro-mechanical coupling denoted as $\Psi_\epsilon$ can be straightforwardly added as an additional term, reading
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:free_energy_density_additive}
	\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A) = \bar\Psi(J,\theta) + \hat\Psi_{\infty}(\F,\theta) + \sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}\hat\Psi_{\alpha}(\F,\theta,\vect{A}_{\alpha}) + \Psi_{\epsilon}(\F,\E_0,\theta) \,.
\end{equation}



Each of the components of the energy density function follow an identical form to that in equation \eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant}, obtained for the case of a single contribution of the energy density function. Therefore, the complete form of the energy density function $\Psi$ is the following
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model}
\begin{aligned}
		\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A) &=
	\underbrace{%
		\bar\Psi_{R}(J) - \bar\eta_{R}(J) \bar\zeta_{R} \left(\bar f(\theta)-1\right)
	}_{\bar\Psi} + \\ 
	&+
		\underbrace{%
		\hat\Psi_{R,\infty}(\vect{F}) - \hat\eta_{R,\infty}(\vect{F}) \hat\zeta_{R,\infty} \left(\hat f(\theta)-1\right) %
	}_{\hat\Psi_{\infty}}\\
	%
	&+	\underbrace{%
		\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha}(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha}) - \hat\eta_{R,\alpha}(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha}) \hat\zeta_{R,\alpha} \left(\hat f_{\alpha}(\theta)-1\right) %
	}_{\hat\Psi_{\alpha}}\\
	%
	 &+	\underbrace{%
	 	\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0) - \eta_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0) \zeta_{R,\epsilon} \left( f_\epsilon(\theta)-1\right) %
	 }_{\Psi_{\epsilon}}
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The specific form for $\{\bar\Psi_R,\hat\Psi_{R,\infty},\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha},\Psi_{R,\epsilon}\}$ and $\{\bar\eta_{R},\hat\eta_{R,\infty},\hat\eta_{R,\alpha},\eta_{R,\epsilon}\}$ will be discussed in the following subsections. The remaining functions, namely $\{\bar f,\hat f_{},\hat f_{\alpha},f_{\epsilon}\}$ will be determined by the calibration analysis carried out in Section \ref{sec:calibration}.


%
%where the choices in sections \ref{sec:volumetric_model}, \ref{sec:viscoelastic_model} and \ref{sec:electro_mech_model} have been substituted, yielding \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model}.



\subsubsection{Volumetric free energy density} \label{sec:volumetric_model}

The volumetric response is governed by the entropy at the reference temperature, $\bar{\eta}_R$, and the internal energy at the reference state, $\bar{\Eps}_R$. Following \cite{BonetGil2026}, these quantities are defined as
%
\begin{align}
	\label{eqn:volumetric_eta}
	\bar\eta_R(J) &=
	c_v^0 \bar\xi_R
	+ c_v^0 \Gamma \frac{J^q-1}{q} \,,
	\\
	\label{eqn:volumetric_Epsilon}
	\bar\Eps_R(J) &=
	U(J) + \beta (J-1) + \bar\Eps_0 \,,
	\qquad
	U(J)=\frac{1}{2}\kappa_R (J-1)^2 \,,
\end{align}
%
where $\Gamma$ denotes the Mie--Gr\"uneisen constant and $q$ is a dimensionless parameter. The functions above are respectively concave and convex with respect to $J$. The volumetric contribution $U(J)$ may be replaced by any other coercive potential whose tangent modulus at $J=1$ coincides with the bulk modulus $\kappa_R$.

The constant $\bar\Eps_0$ is introduced to ensure the positivity of the free-energy density, with minimum admissible value
$\bar\Eps_0 = c_v^0 \bar\xi_R \theta_R$.
The parameter $\beta$ is related to the Mie--Gr\"uneisen constant by enforcing zero pressure in the reference configuration, namely
%
\begin{equation}
	\left.
	\frac{\partial \bar\Psi}{\partial J}
	\right|_{\substack{J=1\\ \theta=\theta_R}}
	=0
	\qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad
	\beta = \Gamma c_v^0 \theta_R \,.
\end{equation}

Moreover, the Mie--Gr\"uneisen constant can be related to the linear thermal expansion coefficient through
%
\begin{equation}
	\left.
	\frac{\partial^2 \bar\Psi}
	{\partial J \, \partial \theta}
	\right|_{\substack{J=1\\ \theta=\theta_R}}
	=
	-3\alpha \kappa_R
	=
	-\Gamma c_v^0 \,.
\end{equation}

Following again \cite{BonetGil2026}, the thermal function $\bar f$ associated with the volumetric contribution is chosen according to the entropic elasticity model in Eq.~\eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_bonet}, which guarantees positivity of the volumetric specific heat.

For the particular case $q=1$, using the relation between $\Psi_R$ and $\Eps_R$ given in Eq.~\eqref{eqn:internal energy reference temperature} and substituting it into Eq.~\eqref{eqn:free_energy_density_integral_variant}, the volumetric free-energy density can be written as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:volumetric_free_energy}
	\boxed{
		\bar\Psi(J,\theta)
		=
		\underbrace{U(J)}_{\bar\Psi_R}
		-
		\underbrace{
			\left(
			\frac{c_v^0}{\bar\gamma}
			+
			3\alpha \kappa_R (J-1)
			\right)
		}_{\bar\eta_R}
		\underbrace{
			\frac{\theta_R}{\bar\gamma+1}
		}_{\bar\zeta_R}
		\left[
		\underbrace{
			\left(
			\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}
			\right)^{\bar\gamma+1}
		}_{\bar f}
		-1
		\right] .
	}
\end{equation}

Following Bonet and Gil \cite{BonetGil2026}, we define the entropy associated with the long-term contribution at the reference temperature as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:eta iso}
	\hat{\eta}_{R,\infty}(\vect{F})
	=
	-\frac{\Psi_{R,\infty}(\vect{F})}{\zeta_{R,\infty}} \, .
\end{equation}

This definition implies that $\hat{\Psi}_R = -\hat{\zeta}_R \hat{\eta}_R$, and therefore the deviatoric free-energy density vanishes at the Kelvin state, namely $\hat{\Psi}_K = 0$ (see \eqref{eqn:kelvin energy}). In addition, the contribution to the specific heat coefficient at the reference configuration is also null, that is,
$\hat{c}_v(\I,\theta_R)=0$.
Substituting \eqref{eqn:eta iso} into the definition of $\hat{\Psi}_{\infty}$ in \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} yields the simplified expression
%
\begin{equation*}\label{eqn:final hat psi infty}
	\boxed{
		\hat{\Psi}_{\infty}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta)
		=
		\hat{f}(\theta)\,\hat{\Psi}_{R,\infty}(\vect{F})
	}
\end{equation*}

In this work, the long-term free-energy density $\hat{\Psi}_{R,\infty}(\vect{F})$ is described by a nonlinear Mooney--Rivlin model,
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:isochoric_mooney_rivlin}
	\hat\Psi_{R,\infty}(\F) =
	\frac{\mu_1}{2\alpha_1 3^{(\alpha_1-1)}}\left((\hat\F\!:\!\hat\F)^{\alpha_1} - 3^{\alpha_1}\right) +
	\frac{\mu_2}{2\alpha_2 3^{(\alpha_2-1)}}\left((\hat\H\!:\!\hat\H)^{\alpha_2} - 3^{\alpha_2}\right),
\end{equation}
where the isochoric deformation gradient and cofactor are given by
%
\begin{equation}
	\hat\F = J^{-\sfrac{1}{3}}\F \,,
	\qquad
	\hat\H = J^{-\sfrac{2}{3}}\H \,.
\end{equation}

The rationale for adopting this constitutive model is discussed in Section \ref{sec:calibration}.

Finally, the choice of the thermal law $\hat{f}(\theta)$ is motivated by the experimental evidence presented in Section \ref{sec:calibration}. In particular, a nonlinear melting-type function, defined in \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting}, is adopted for $\hat{f}(\theta)$.


\subsubsection{Non-equilibrium isochoric free energy density} \label{sec:viscoelastic_model}


Analogously to the long-term isochoric contribution, the entropy associated with the non-equilibrium response at the reference temperature is defined as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:eta alpha}
	\hat{\eta}_{R,\alpha}(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha})
	=
	-\frac{\hat{\Psi}_{R,\alpha}(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha})}
	{\hat{\zeta}_{R,\alpha}} \, .
\end{equation}

Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eqn:eta alpha} into the definition of $\hat{\Psi}_{\alpha}$ given in Eq.~\eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} leads to the simplified expression
%
\begin{equation}	\label{eqn:final hat psi alpha}
	\boxed{		\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}
		\hat{\Psi}_{\alpha}
		(\vect{F},\theta,\vect{A}_{\alpha})
		=
		\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}\hat{f}_{\alpha}(\theta)\,

		(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha})
	}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Electro-mechanical coupling} \label{sec:electro_mech_model}

Finally, the electro-mechanical coupling term $\Psi_{\epsilon}$ is essential for modelling the behaviour of Electro-Active Polymers (EAPs). In these materials, the electric state interacts with mechanical deformation, potentially inducing significant thermal fluctuations.
The same hypothesis in \ref{sec:viscoelastic_model} holds, namely we propose the following definition of $\eta_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0)$
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:etaR epsilon}
	\eta_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0)=-\frac{\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0)}{\zeta_{R,\epsilon}}
\end{equation}

Introduction of \eqref{eqn:etaR epsilon} in the definition of $\Psi_{\epsilon}$ in \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model}
leads to the following simplified expression for $\Psi_{\epsilon}$, namely
%
\begin{equation*}\label{eqn:final psi epsilon}
\boxed{
	\Psi_{\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0,\theta)=f_{\epsilon}(\theta)\,\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0)
	}
\end{equation*}

The purely electro-mechanical energy follows the commonly expression of an ideal dielectric elastomer, namely
% The use of polyconvex thermodynamic potentials in this part of the model is crucial to ensure numerical stability and the physical realism of the solutions in large strain regimes.
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:electro_mech_coupling_ref}
	\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\F,\E_0) = -\frac{\varepsilon_r \varepsilon_0}{2J}\H\E_0 \cdot \H\E_0 \,,
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_0$ is the vacuum permittivity and $\varepsilon_r$ is the relative permittivity of the material. The thermal coupling function $f_\epsilon$ will be defined in the later section \ref{sec:calibration} based on experimental evidence, which shows a smooth change in the electro-mechanical coupling with temperature.



\subsubsection{Final form of the energy density function}


Finally, substituting the expressions for
$\{\bar\Psi,\hat\Psi_{\infty},\hat\Psi_{\alpha},\Psi_{\epsilon}\}$
given in Eqs.~\eqref{eqn:volumetric_free_energy},
\eqref{eqn:final hat psi infty},
\eqref{eqn:final hat psi alpha}, and
\eqref{eqn:final psi epsilon},
respectively, into
Eq.~\eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model},
the  energy density function
$\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A)$
can be rewritten as
%
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:compact form energy}
	\boxed{			\begin{aligned}
			\Psi(\F,\E_0,\theta,\A) &=
			\underbrace{%
				\bar\Psi_R(J) - \bar\eta_R(J) \bar\zeta_R \left(\bar f(\theta)-1\right) \vphantom{\frac{A}{B}}%
			}_{\bar\Psi} + 
			\underbrace{%
			\hat f_\infty(\theta)	\hat\Psi_{R,\infty}(\F) }_{{\hat\Psi}}\\&+ \sum_\alpha^{n_\text{Maxw}} \hat f_\alpha(\theta) \underbrace{\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha}(\F,\Aa) %
			}_{\hat\Psi_{\alpha}} +
			\underbrace{%
			f_\epsilon(\theta)	\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\F,\E_0)  \vphantom{\sum_{a}^{n_M}}%
			}_{\Psi_\epsilon} .
	\end{aligned}}
\end{equation}

This compact representation constitutes the form of the energy density adopted throughout the present work.

\subsection{Incompressible viscoelasticity at finite strains}
\label{sec:incompressible_viscoelasticity}

Following the work in \cite{Liu2025}, this Section presents the theoretical framework for large strain inccompressible viscoelasticity, where the component $\Psi_{R,\alpha}(\vect{F},\vect{A}_{\alpha})$ will be specified. Furthermore, so far, the internal variables $\vect{A}_{\alpha}$ for every Maxwell branch $\alpha=\{1,\dots,n_{\text{Maxw}}\}$ and suitable evolution laws for them. 

\subsubsection{Continuum kinematics and multiplicative decomposition}
\label{sec:viscous_kinematics_multiplicative}

\begin{figure}
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/multiplicative_decomposition.pdf}
	\caption{Multiplicative decomposition of the deformation gradient.}
	\label{fig:2split}
\end{figure}


As customary in finite-strain viscoelasticity, the deformation gradient $\F$ is multiplicatively decomposed into elastic and viscous contributions \cite{Kroner1959,Lee1969elastic,Anand1985constitutive,Bergstrom1998}
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:multiplicative_F}
	\F = \F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha},
\end{equation}
%
through the introduction of suitable intermediate stress-free configurations (see Fig.~\ref{fig:2split}). Here, $\alpha=1,\dots,n_{\text{Maxw}}$ labels the viscous branches of the generalised Maxwell model. The tensor $\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ represents the internal variable associated with branch $\alpha$, characterising the current viscous state of the material. Thermodynamic equilibrium is recovered when $\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}=\F$.
To ensure objectivity, the following right Cauchy--Green deformation tensors are introduced:
%
\begin{subequations}
	\label{eqn:Cauchy-Green}
	\begin{equation}
		\C=\F^{\mathrm{T}}\F,
		\qquad
		\C_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}
		=
		\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{\mathrm{T}}
		\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}.
		\tag{\ref*{eqn:Cauchy-Green}a,b}
	\end{equation}
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:Cauchy-Creen_a}%
	\refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:Cauchy-Creen_b}%
\end{subequations}
%
Here, $\C$ and $\C_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ denote the total and viscous right Cauchy--Green tensors, respectively. In contrast, the elastic tensor
$\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}
=
\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{\mathrm{T}}
\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}$
is defined in the intermediate configuration and is therefore not rotation free. Indeed, the multiplicative decomposition \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_F} contains an arbitrary intermediate rotation, such that
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:F_defcomposition}
	\F
	=
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*}
	\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{*},
	\qquad
	\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{*}
	=
	\boldsymbol{Q}\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha},
	\qquad
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*}
	=
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}\boldsymbol{Q}^{\mathrm{T}},
\end{equation}
%
where $\boldsymbol{Q}\in\mathrm{SO}(3)$ is an arbitrary rotation tensor. Following \cite{Holthusen2023}, this redundancy is removed by selecting
$\boldsymbol{Q}=\boldsymbol{R}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{\mathrm{T}}$,
where $\boldsymbol{R}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ stems from the polar decomposition
%
\[
\F_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}
=
\boldsymbol{R}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}
\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}.
\]
%
This yields the unique decomposition
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqb:Fv_rotation_free}
	\F
	=
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*}
	\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha},
	\qquad
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*}
	=
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}
	\boldsymbol{R}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha},
\end{equation}
%
from which the elastic right Cauchy--Green tensor follows as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:Cea}
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}
	=
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*\mathrm{T}}
	\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}^{*}
	=
	\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{-1}
	\C
	\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
%
Consequently, the viscous stretch tensor $\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ is uniquely defined and fully characterises the viscous deformation state.

\subsubsection{Strain energy function}
\label{sec:strain_energy_function}

To guarantee that $\hat{\Psi}_\alpha$ and hence $\hat{\Psi}_{R,\alpha}$ \eqref{eqn:final hat psi alpha}  vanishes when $\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ and $\F$ only differ by a rigid body rotation (i.e. thermo-dynamical equilibrium), \cite{Liu2025} particularise the definition of $\Psi_\alpha$  as 
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:Psi_Cea}
	\hat{\Psi}_{R,\alpha}(\F,\Aa) =
	\widetilde{\hat{\Psi}}_{R,\alpha}(\C,\U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}) =
	\widetilde{\hat{\Psi}}_{R,\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}(\hat{\C}_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha});\qquad \hat{\C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} = J_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-\frac{2}{3}}\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}},
\end{equation}
%
where $\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}$ is defined in \eqref{eqn:Cea}, and the notation $\widetilde{\Psi}$ is introduced to emphasise the alternative functional dependence of the free-energy density. For the particular case of isotropic viscoelasticity, a possible choice for $\hat{\Psi}_{R,\alpha}(\F,\Aa)$ is an incompressible Neo-Hookean-type model of the form
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:isochoric_visco_neo_hokean}
	\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha}(\F,\Aa)
	=
	\widetilde{\hat{\Psi}}_{R,\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}(\hat\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha})
	=
	\frac{\mu_\alpha}{2}
	\left(
	\hat\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}\!:\!\hat\F_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha} - 3
	\right)=\frac{\mu_\alpha}{2}
	\left(
	\tr\left(\hat\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}\right) - 3
	\right),
\end{equation}
%
with $\mu_\alpha$ denoting the shear modulus associated with the $\alpha$-th viscoelastic branch.

\subsubsection{First and second Piola-Kirchhoff stress tensor}
\label{sec:visco_first_second_Piola}


With these ingredients, the total first Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor $\P$ introduced in \eqref{eqn:constitutive 1} can be obtained. By exploiting the additive decomposition of the free-energy function in \eqref{eqn:compact form energy}, the stress tensor admits the decomposition
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:visco_first_piola}
	\P
	=
	\left.{\partial_{\F}}\Psi\right|_{\vect{A}_{\alpha}=\text{const}}
	=
	\bar\P + \hat\P_\infty + \sum_{\alpha}\hat\P_\alpha + \P_\epsilon ,
\end{equation}
%
where the volumetric, long-term isochoric, non-equilibrium isochoric, and thermo-mechanical contributions are respectively given by
%
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\bar\P
		&=
		{\partial_{\F}}\bar\Psi
		=
		\left[
		\bar\Psi_R^{\prime}(J)
		-
		\bar\eta_R^{\prime}(J)\,\bar\zeta_R
		\left(\bar f(\theta)-1\right)
		\right]\vect{H},
		&\qquad
		\hat\P_\infty
		&=
		\hat{f}(\theta)\,
		{\partial_{\F}}\hat\Psi_{R,\infty},
		\\
		%
		\hat\P_\alpha
		&=
		\hat{f}_{\alpha}(\theta)
		\left.
		{\partial_{\F}}\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha}
		\right|_{\vect{U}_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}=\text{const}},
		&\qquad
		\P_\epsilon
		&=
		{f}_{\epsilon}(\theta)\,
		{\partial_{\F}}\Psi_\epsilon .
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
%
Although the proposed framework is formulated primarily in terms of the first Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor, the viscoelastic contribution is more naturally expressed through the second Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor. For a single Maxwell branch, the elastic second Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor associated with the viscous contribution is defined as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:maxw_Sea}
	\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}
	=
	2 \hat{f}_{\alpha}(\theta)\,
	{\partial_{\C_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}}
		\widetilde{\hat{\Psi}}_{\alpha}
		\left(\hat{\C}_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}\right)} .
\end{equation}
%
Making use of the relationship
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:Sea_Sa}
	D\left.\Psi_{\alpha}\right|_{\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}=\textrm{const}}[\delta \C]
	=
	D \widetilde{\Psi}_{\alpha}
	\left[
	D\left.\C_{\textrm{e}_{\alpha}}\right|_{\boldsymbol{U}_{\textrm{v}_{\alpha}}=\textrm{const}}
	[\delta \C]
	\right],
\end{equation}
%
it is possible to derive (see \cite{Liu2025}) the relationship between the viscous second Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor $\hat{\vect{S}}_{\alpha}$ and its elastic counterpart $\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}$, namely
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:Sea_Uva}
	\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}_{\alpha}
	=
	\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{-1}
	\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}
	\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}^{-1},
	\qquad
	\hat{\vect{P}}_{\alpha}
	=
	\vect{F}\hat{\vect{S}}_{\alpha}.
\end{equation}
%
This expression establishes the connection between the Maxwell viscous contribution to the second Piola--Kirchhoff stress tensor and the corresponding elastic stress measure.

For completeness, in addition to the stress tensor $\vect{P}$, the electric displacement field $\vect{D}_0$ and the entropy $\eta$ introduced in \eqref{eqn:constitutive 1} are reported for the compact free-energy representation \eqref{eqn:compact form energy}. The electric displacement field is given by
%
\begin{equation}
	\vect{D}_0
	=
	-f_{\epsilon}(\theta)\,
	\partial_{\vect{E}_0}
	\Psi_{R,\epsilon}(\vect{F},\vect{E}_0),
\end{equation}
%
whereas the entropy reads
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:visco_entropy}
	\eta
	=
	-\left.
	{\partial_{\theta}}\Psi
	\right|_{\vect{A}_{\alpha}=\text{const}}
	=
	\bar\eta
	+
	\hat\eta_\infty
	+
	\sum_{\alpha}\hat\eta_\alpha
	+
	\eta_\epsilon .
\end{equation}
%
The individual entropy contributions are obtained as
%
\begin{equation}
	\begin{aligned}
		\bar\eta
		&=
		\bar\eta_R(J)\,
		\bar\zeta_R\,
		\bar f^{\prime}(\theta),
		&\qquad
		\hat\eta_\infty
		&=
		-\hat{f}^{\prime}(\theta)\,
		\hat\Psi_{R,\infty},
		\\
		%
		\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}\hat\eta_{\alpha}
		&=
		-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{n_{\text{Maxw}}}
		\hat{f}_{\alpha}^{\prime}(\theta)\,
		\hat\Psi_{R,\alpha},
		&\qquad
		\eta_{\epsilon}
		&=
		-
		{f}_{\epsilon}^{\prime}(\theta)\,
		\Psi_{R,\epsilon}.
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Volume-preserving constraints}
\label{sec:volume-preserving_constraints}

The incompressibility of the viscous stretch tensor $\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}$ implies that
$\det \boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}} = 1$. Recalling the multiplicative decomposition introduced in \eqref{eqn:multiplicative_F}, this condition can alternatively be expressed as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:visco_Je}
	J_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	\det \F_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	\det \F
	=
	J .
\end{equation}
%
Taking the time derivative of the kinematic constraint \eqref{eqn:visco_Je} yields
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:visco_Je_t}
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}
	:
	\left.
	\frac{\mathrm{d}\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}}{\mathrm{d}t}
	\right|_{\F=\text{const}}
	=
	0,
\end{equation}
%
which provides a kinematic constraint governing the evolution of the elastic right Cauchy--Green tensor $\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$.

It is worth noting that the viscous contribution is formulated in terms of the isochoric tensor $\hat{\vect{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ (see \eqref{eqn:isochoric_visco_neo_hokean}), which directly implies the deviatoric condition
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:deviatoricS}
	\hat{\S}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	:
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	0.
\end{equation}
%
This relation may be interpreted as an alternative kinetic, i.e.\ stress-based, incompressibility constraint equivalent to \eqref{eqn:visco_Je} or \eqref{eqn:visco_Je_t}\footnote{This kinetic constraint follows from the homogeneous nature of degree zero of the Maxwell strain-energy function $\hat{\widetilde{\Psi}}_{\alpha}$, namely $\hat{\widetilde{\Psi}}_{\alpha}(\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}})=\hat{\widetilde{\Psi}}_{\alpha}(\beta \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}})$ for any arbitrary constant $\beta$; see \cite{Bonet_book_statics}.}. Further differentiation of this constraint, together with the use of \eqref{eqn:visco_Je_t}, leads to the additional incompressibility condition
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{const_tensor}
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	:
	\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	:
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	0,
	\qquad
	\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	2
	{\partial_{\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}}}
	\hat{\S}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}},
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ denotes the fourth-order elasticity tensor. Owing to its deviatoric character, the tensor $\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ is not strictly positive definite (see \eqref{const_tensor}) and, consequently, cannot be directly inverted. 
A simple strategy to recover invertibility consists of introducing the following additive regularisation:
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:4-order-Ce}
	\bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	=
	\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}
	+
	m\,
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}
	\otimes
	\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1},
\end{equation}
%
where $m$ is an arbitrary positive parameter acting as an artificial bulk modulus. This extension guarantees the positive definiteness, and therefore the invertibility, of the enhanced elasticity tensor $\bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$.



\subsubsection{Viscous dissipation and evolution law}\label{sec::dissipation_law}

To completely specify the proposed viscoelastic model, an evolution law for the set of internal variables $\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$, with $\alpha = 1,\ldots,n_{\alpha}$, must be introduced. Following the framework proposed in \cite{Liu2025}, the evolution equations are constructed so as to guarantee \textit{ab initio} positive viscous dissipation. Accordingly, the rate of energy dissipation associated with viscous effects is defined as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:dvis_inequality}
	\begin{aligned}
		\Dvis &= - \sum_{\alpha} {{\partial_{ \boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}}} \Psi_\alpha}{} : \dot{\boldsymbol{U}}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha} \\
		&=-\sum_{\alpha} \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\Psi_\alpha \left(\C,~\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha} \right)}{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} \\
		&= -\sum_{\alpha} \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\widetilde{\Psi}_\alpha \left(\hat{\C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}\left(\C,~\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha} \right)\right)}{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} \\
		&=-\sum_{\alpha} \frac{1}{2}\hat{\boldsymbol{S}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}{\C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} \left(\C,~\boldsymbol{U}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha} \right)}{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}},
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
%

To ensure that the evolution law is compatible with the second law of thermodynamics \cite{Gurtin2010}, the condition $\Dvis \geq 0$ must be satisfied regardless of the current state of deformation and stress. In order to achieve this, note firstly that
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq23}
	\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} =
    2 \bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}},
\end{equation}
%
where $\bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ denotes the enhanced fourth-order Lagrangian elasticity tensor introduced in \eqref{eqn:4-order-Ce}, which is positive definite by construction. Importantly, for standard constitutive models based on simple strain-energy densities, such as Saint-Venant or Neo-Hookean formulations, both $\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ and its inverse $\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}$ can be derived in closed form in a straightforward manner; see \cite{Bonet_book_statics}.%
%Note that the fourth order tensor $\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ is generally positive definite. Typical examples are the elasticity tensor of the simple Saint-Venant–Kirchhoff material model, which is given by
%%
%\begin{equation}
%	\label{eq25}
%	\mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{\mathrm{SVK}} = \lambda \boldsymbol{I} \otimes \boldsymbol{I} + \mu \left(\mathbb{L} + \mathbb{L}^{\mathrm{T}}  \right); \quad \mathbb{L}_{IJKL} = \delta_{IK} \delta_{JL}; \quad \mathbb{L}_{IJKL}^{\mathrm{T}} = \delta_{IL} \delta_{JK},
%\end{equation}
%%

%%

%\end{equation}

Using Eq.~\eqref{eq23}, the internal viscous dissipation can now be rewritten as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq28}
	\Dvis = -\sum_{\alpha} \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \mathbb{C}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}},
\end{equation}
%
where in order to ensure positive dissipation, the following general evolution law is proposed
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:evol_law}
	\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{S}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} =
    -\frac{1}{\tau_\alpha} \left( 
      \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} -\lambda_\alpha \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}
    \right); \quad\quad
  \tau_\alpha > 0 \,.
\end{equation}
%
The relaxation time $\tau_\alpha$  determines the rate of dissipation of the viscous stresses, and the last term in \eqref{eqn:evol_law} represents a possible a possible volumetric contribution,  guaranteeing the incompressibility. Using Eqs. \eqref{eqn:visco_Je_t} and \eqref{eq23} along with above Eq. \eqref{eqn:evol_law}, the value of the scalar parameter $\lambda_\alpha$ (i.e. Lagrange multiplier) can be obtained as
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:Lagrange_multiplier}
	\lambda_\alpha = \frac{\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}}{\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}}.
\end{equation}


For completeness, by the use of Eqs.~\eqref{eqn:evol_law} and \eqref{eqn:Lagrange_multiplier}, the viscous dissipation can finally take the form of
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq68}
	\begin{aligned}
		\Dvis &= -\sum_{\alpha} \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} \\
		&=\sum_{\alpha} \frac{1}{\tau_\alpha} \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \left( \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} - \frac{\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}}{\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}} \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} \right) \\
		& = \sum_{\alpha} \frac{1}{\tau_\alpha} \frac{ (\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}})(\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}) - (\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1})^2 }{\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1} : \bar{\mathbb{C}}^{-1}_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} : \C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{-1}} \\
		&\geq 0,
	\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

and the positive value of the numerator is a consequence of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.

\subsubsection{Incremental evolution law in the discrete setting}
\label{sec:incremental_evolution_law}

For the sake of computational implementation, Eq.~\eqref{eqn:evol_law} would need to be integrated in time, where the update of the state variables $\U_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ from time step $n$ to time step $n+1$ is implicit. To transform the left-hand side of Eq.~\eqref{eqn:evol_law} into an incremental form, a classical (first order) backward Euler scheme renders
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:incremental_evol_law}
	\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} }{\mathrm{d}t} \right|_{\F=\text{const}} =
    \frac{1}{\Delta t} \left(
      \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} - \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{*}
    \right),
\end{equation}
%
where $\Delta t$ denotes the time step size, and the stress tensors $\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ and $\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{*}$ are defined by
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:intermediate_S}
	\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{*} = \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} \left(\C^{n+1},~\U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}^n \right)
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} = \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}} \left(\C^{n+1},~\U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} \right),
\end{equation}
%
where the upper script $n$ and $n+1$ indicate evaluation in their corresponding time steps. Note that $\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{*}$ represents the state of stress attained from the assumption that there is no viscous deformation taking place from time step $n$ to $n+1$. Alternatively, it can also be interpreted as the state of stress attained when the deformation from time step $n$ to $n+1$ takes place instantaneously\footnote{In the case of elastoplasticity, this term is known as the trial stress that may or may not satisfy the yield inequality.}. In the context of viscoelasticity, the stress relaxation is inevitable and the final stress can be derived by substituting \eqref{eqn:incremental_evol_law} into \eqref{eqn:evol_law}, resulting in
%
\begin{equation}
	\label{eqn:backward_euler_S}
	\frac{1}{\Delta t} \left( \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} -  \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{*} \right) =
  - \frac{1}{\tau_\alpha} \left(
    \hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} - \lambda_\alpha^{n+1} \left(C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}\right)^{-1}
  \right) .
\end{equation}
Once the final relaxation stress $\hat\S_{mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ is computed, the elastic right-Cauchy-Green strain tensor $\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ can be determined making use of the user-defined strain energy density function $\widetilde{\Psi}_{\alpha}$. After obtaining $\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$, the corresponding viscous state variable $\U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ can be derived from \eqref{eqn:Cea} as
%
\begin{subequations}
  \label{eqn:Uva_n1}
  \begin{equation}
    \U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} = \left(\U_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}\right)^{-1} \left(
      \U_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} \C^{n+1} \U_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}
    \right)^{\sfrac{1}{2}}
    \left(\U_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}\right)^{-1} \,;\quad\quad
    \U_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} = \left(\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}\right)^{\sfrac{1}{2}}
    \tag{\ref*{eqn:Uva_n1}a,b}
  \end{equation}
  \refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:Uva_n1_a}%
  \refstepcounter{equation}\label{eqn:Uva_n1_b}%
\end{subequations}


As for the Lagrange multiplier $\lambda_\alpha^{n+1}$, rather than using Eq. \eqref{eqn:Lagrange_multiplier}, which only enforces the rate form of the incompressibility constraint\footnote{This is similar to classical time integration return mapping approaches in elastoplasticity \cite{Simo1988,Simo1992associative} for the computation of the algorithmic tangent modulus.}, $\lambda_\alpha^{n+1}$ can be incrementally obtained by enforcing the constraint
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:incremental_lambda}
  \det\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1} = \left(J^{n+1}\right)^2.
\end{equation}

Notably, Eqs. \eqref{eqn:backward_euler_S} and \eqref{eqn:incremental_lambda} represent an implicit system of nonlinear equations for the solution of $\U^{n+1}_{\textrm{v}_{\alpha}}$ and $\lambda_{\alpha}^{n+1}$ in terms of $\textbf{C}^{n+1}$ and $\U^n_{\textrm{v}_{\alpha}}$. The system can be solved provided $\textbf{C}^{n+1}$ and $\U^n_{\textrm{v}_{\alpha}}$, Eqs.~\eqref{eqn:backward_euler_S} and \eqref{eqn:incremental_lambda} can be first solved in terms of $\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ and $\lambda_{\alpha}^{n+1}$ prior definition of the trial stress tensor $\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}$ and with knowledge of the Maxwell viscous strain energy density $\hat{\tilde{\Psi}}_{\alpha}$. Subsequently, the viscous stretch tensor $\U^{n+1}_{\textrm{v}_{\alpha}}$ can be evaluated by using Eq.~\eqref{eqn:Uva_n1}. The numerical procedure for the calculation of the internal variable $\U_{\mathrm{v}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ is outlined in Algorithm \ref{alg:incompressible_internal_variable}.

\begin{algorithm}
	\caption{Numerical procedure for the calculation of the internal variable in the incompressible case.}
  \label{alg:incompressible_internal_variable}
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\For {$Time\; Increment=1,2,\ldots$}
		\For {$Iteration=1,2,\ldots$}
		\State Calculate the right Cauchy-Green strain tensor at the current time increment $\C^{n+1}$
		\State Compute the instantaneous stress tensor $\hat\S_{\mathrm{e}_\alpha}$
		\State Obtain the elastic right Cauchy-Green tensor $\C_{\mathrm{e}_{\alpha}}^{n+1}$ by the solution of Eq.~\eqref{eqn:backward_euler_S} and Eq.~\eqref{eqn:incremental_lambda}
		\State Determine the internal variable $\U^{n+1}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ according to Eq.~\eqref{eqn:Uva_n1}
		\EndFor
		\State Store the internal variable $\U^{n+1}_{\mathrm{v}_\alpha}$ and $\lambda_\alpha^{n+1}$ as history variable
		\EndFor
	\end{algorithmic} 
\end{algorithm}
