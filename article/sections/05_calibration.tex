
The constitutive model presented in section \ref{sec:constitutive equations} is general and can be applied to a wide variety of materials. In the context of EAPs, multiple authors have performed experimental testing, of the single physics, such as mechanical, thermal or electrical behaviour \cite{Hossain2012visco,Dippel2015} as well as the coupled behaviour of the combination of two \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020,Alkhoury2022} or the three physics \cite{Mehnert2021a,Sheng2012,Qin2023}.
The extensive experimental data for VHB polymer has motivated the choice of this material to validate the constitutive model, which involves not only fitting the best parameters, but the choice of specific mechanical models as well as thermodynamically-consistent coupling functions.

Beyond finding optimal parameters, the scope of the calibration is to validate the theoretical formulation and to show its capabilities. The proposed framework is general and the conclusions related to the methodology can be applied for other materials characterization.

\subsubsection{Data consistency}

However, the methodology followed by different authors to perform experiments is not standardized and the data reported in the literature is not always consistent.
For instance, the same type of experiment performed by different authors under the same conditions of temperature, velocity and stretch, may report different stresses.
There are several potential sources for the differences of the measured properties: VHB polymer is manufactured as an adhesive tape, hence, quality control focus on properties different from viscoelastic behaviour; VHB polymer is provided in different thicknesses, which can alter the curing process of the polymer; and finally, engineering tolerances are not defined for the current properties and can suffer variations from one batch of production to another one.

This observation would open a discussion about uncertainty of the material data and error propagation. Given the scope of this investigation is to assess the suitability of the numerical model to predict the behaviour of EAPs, only consistent data will be considered and table \ref{tab:experiment_sources} reports discarded datasets.



\subsection{Reduced constitutive equations for experimental configurations}

The tests presented in table \ref{tab:experiment_sources} can be described with simple kinematics and measures, allowing parameter identification. This section describes the reduced kinematics and the measures of energy derivatives (stress, specific heat, dielectric constant) for the different physics.


\subsubsection{Thermal expansion and Jacobian}
The calorimetry tests as well as the thermo-mechanical tests are exposed to thermal variation. At the initial configuration, in the absence of external pressure, there will be a volume change without deviatoric deformation gradient.
In such situation, Eq. \eqref{eqn:volumetric_free_energy} yields the following relation for the Jacobian as a function of the temperature,
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:thermal_Jacobian}
    p = \frac{\partial\bar\Psi}{\partial J} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    J(\theta) = 1 + 3\alpha\frac{\theta}{\bar\gamma+1}
    \left(\left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\bar\gamma+1} - 1\right).
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Mechanical tests kinematics}
The deformation gradient of a mechanical test in the absence of thermal variation and in the absence of electric field is given by
\begin{equation}
  \F_M = \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & \lambda^{-1/2} & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & \lambda^{-1/2} \\
  \end{bmatrix} \,,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the stretch in the loading direction according to engineering notation. The deformation gradient is purely isochoric and the volumetric part is given by $J=1$.


\subsubsection{First Piola-Kirchhoff stress}

The process of the calibration of a constitutive model involves the simulation of the experiments and how the constitutive model is able to predict the stress for the series of stretches in each experiment. The aforementioned deformation gradients define the kinematics of the specimen and the evaluation of the stress must be closed with appropriate boundary conditions (i.e., a traction-free state on the lateral surfaces of the specimen) by the addition of a hydrostatic pressure. The total Piola-Kirchhoff stress is given by $\P = \hat\P - pJ\F^{-T}$. Given that the lateral components must vanish ($P_{22} = P_{33} = 0$), the pressure $p$ is coupled to the constitutive response as:
\begin{equation}
  p = \hat P_{22} F_{22}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the axial stress used in the objective function of the model calibration is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  P_{11} = \hat P_{11} - pF_{11}^{-1} = \hat P_{11} - \hat P_{22} \frac{F_{22}}{F_{11}}
\end{equation}


\subsubsection{Thermo-mechanical kinematics}

The mechanical tests reported in \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020,Alkhoury2022} describe the following protocol: First, the specimens were placed inside the chamber and conditioned at the target temperature for fifteen minutes to achieve thermal homogeneity across the \si{0.5}{mm} thickness. Crucially, the specimens were mounted onto the grippers only after this thermal stabilization period. The protective silicon paper was then removed gently to ensure the test initiated without any pre-tension. Consequently, the reference configuration for each experimentâ€”corresponding to a stretch of $\lambda=1$ and zero nominal stressâ€”is defined by the stress-free state of the material after free thermal expansion or contraction at the specific test temperature. The mechanical response was recorded as the Piola (nominal) stress, calculated by dividing the applied force by the initial undeformed cross-sectional area measured at the start of the test.
Accordingly, the deformation gradient tensor is straightforwardly found as the composition of volumetric and isochoric parts as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:F_thermo_mech_1}
  \F_{TM} = \bar\F \hat\F = J^{1/3} \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & \lambda^{-1/2} & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & \lambda^{-1/2} \\
  \end{bmatrix} \,,
\end{equation}
where $J$ is determined by Eq. \eqref{eqn:thermal_Jacobian}.


\subsubsection{Thermo-electro-mechanical kinematics}

As stated before, thermo-electro-mechanical tests are not standardized and different loading protocols may be reported by authors. In \cite{Mehnert2021a}, the mounted specimen was placed in the temperature-controlled chamber and allowed to reach thermal equilibrium for 15 minutes before loading. The loading sequence was strictly defined: first, a constant electric potential (2 kV to 6 kV) was applied across the thickness. The material was held under this pure electrical load for one minute to stabilize initial deformations. Due to the electric-field-induced expansion being constrained by the fixed clamps, the force measured at the nominal $0\%$ stretch typically initiated at a slight compressive (negative) value. In the absence of electrical field, the deformation gradient would be expressed according to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:F_thermo_mech_2}
  \F_{TM2} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & (J/\lambda)^{1/2} & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & (J/\lambda)^{1/2} \\
  \end{bmatrix} .
\end{equation}
It is important to remark that, unlike \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_mech_1}, \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_mech_2} is related with non-zero axial stresses for $\lambda=1$ at temperatures different from $\theta_R$. Given the electrical field will introduce a transverse deformation $\lambda_2$, the kinematics will follow the expressions
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:F_thermo_electro_mech}
  \F_{TEM} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & J^{1/2}\lambda_2 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & J^{1/2}(\lambda\lambda_2)^{-1} \\
  \end{bmatrix} , \quad
  \E = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ V/T_0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $T_0$ is the reference undeformed thickness and $\lambda_2$ must be such that the transverse stresses $P_{22}$ and $P_{33}$ equal to zero.



\subsection{Methodology: multi-step optimization strategy}

The non-linearity and different physics of the model, as well as the large number of parameters to be calibrated, make the optimization problem very complex. To overcome this issue, a multi-step optimization strategy is proposed. The calibration process is divided into several steps, where each step focuses on a specific subset of parameters and a specific type of experiment. The results of each step are used as constant parameters for the next steps.

At each step, different types of experiments are involved. Each experiment defines a measure $y$, namely, specific heat, stress or dielectric constant at specific kinematics $x_\text{data}$. The difference on the measured $y_\text{data}$ and the predicted $y_\text{pred}$ at the sampling points $x_\text{data}$ will allow to identify the optimal parameters minimizing the error. Table \ref{tab:calibration_steps} summarizes the steps described in this section.

\subsubsection{Step 1: Thermal properties}

The specific heat capacity is calibrated from the DSC experiment, as well as the thermal coupling function, where the specimen is at an unstressed configuration $p=0$. Differentiation of the thermo-electro-mechanical model in Eq. \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} allows to express the specific heat capacity as
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:calibration_cv}
  c_v(\theta) =
    -\theta \left. \frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial \theta^2} \right|_{\substack{\F=\bar\F\hfill\\ \E_0=\mathbf{0}}} =
    \left(c_v^0 + 3\alpha\kappa_R\bar\gamma(J-1)\right)
    \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\bar\gamma} ,
\end{equation}
where the dependence on deviatoric terms vanishes.
At the reference configuration $\F=\I$ and $\theta=\theta_R$, expression \eqref{eqn:calibration_cv} will simply provide the reference specific heat capacity $c_v^0$. However, the presence of thermal variation and thermal expansion will induce variations on the specific heat capacity, according to \cite{BonetGil2026}, thus, it allows to indirectly determining the bulk modulus $\kappa_R$.

The reference temperature is conveniently set to $\SI{20}{\celsius}$, which is the room temperature at which several mechanical tests are performed.
It is important to remark that Eq.'s \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} and \eqref{eqn:calibration_cv} are state functions, hence, the calibrated value of $\bar\gamma$ is valid for any reference temperature $\theta_R$, while the value of $c_v^0$ is only valid for the specific reference temperature $\theta_R$. Different specific heat at reference temperatures $\theta_{R_A}$ and $\theta_{R_B}$ can be related by the expression
\begin{equation}
  c_{v_B}^0 = c_{v_A} (\I,\theta_{R_B}) = c_{v_A}^0 \left(\frac{\theta_{R_B}}{\theta_{R_A}}\right)^{\bar\gamma+1} \,.
\end{equation}



\subsubsection{Step 2: Hyperelastic characterization}

The work presented in \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020} states that the stresses reported in the quasi-static one-cycle loading-unloading tests are close to the asymptotic stress of the creep tests at constant stretch. This observation allows to calibrate the hyperelastic response of the material from the quasi-static test, where the viscous effects are negligible. The hyperelastic response is calibrated by fitting the parameters of $\hat\Psi_{R,\infty}$, which is defined as a non-linear Mooney-Rivlin model in Eq. \eqref{eqn:isochoric_mooney_rivlin} against the quasi-static tests at $\SI{20}{\celsius}$.



\subsubsection{Step 3: Viscoelastic characterization}

The calibration is performed by fitting the loading-unloading tests at the reference temperature of $\SI{20}{\celsius}$, where the viscous effects are significant.
The viscoelastic response is calibrated by fitting the parameters of $\mu_\alpha$ and $\tau_\alpha$ for each viscous branch $\alpha=1,\dots,n_\text{Maxw}$, where the number of branches $n$ is not known a priori. The tangent parameter $\mu_\alpha$ corresponds to the neo-Hookean model in Eq. \eqref{eqn:isochoric_visco_neo_hokean}, and $\tau_n$ is the relaxation time.

Regarding the objective variables, a logarithmic transform has been applied to the relaxation times $\tau_\alpha$ to ensure the positivity of the parameters and to improve the convergence of the optimization algorithm. The optimization is performed in the logarithmic space for $\tau_\alpha$ and in the linear space for $\mu_\alpha$. The number of branches $n$ is determined by analysing the sensitivity of the optimal parameters and the goodness of fit with respect to the number of branches.

% \paragraph{Kinematics} The same kinematics as in the hyperelastic characterization is used for the viscoelastic characterization, where the deformation gradient is given by $\F_M$ and the volumetric part is $J=1$. However, the viscous deformation gradient must be computed by integrating the evolution equation in Eq. \eqref{eqn:viscous_deformation_gradient_evolution}, being the initial viscous strain $U_{v_\alpha}^0=\I$.


\subsubsection{Step 4: Thermo-mechanical coupling characterization}

Thermo-mechanical coupling is calibrated by fitting the full set of loading-unloading experiments. It involves fitting the thermal laws in \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting} and \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_softening} for the hyperelastic response and the viscous response respectively. In the results section it will be discussed the sensitivity of the parameters, that justify using a single thermal function for all the viscous branches.


\subsubsection{Step 5: Dielectric properties}

The BDS experiments in \cite{Sheng2012} provide the measure of the dielectric permittivity $\varepsilon$ at a wide range of frequencies and different temperatures. A frequency of $\SI{1e1}{Hz}$ has been selected for the calibration, which is closer to the low-frequency experiments in \cite{Mehnert2021a}, allowing to focus on the thermo-mechanic coupling. Substitution of \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} and \eqref{eqn:electro_mech_coupling_ref} into the definition of the dielectric permittivity yields
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:bds_epsilon_general}
  \varepsilon(\F,\E_0,\theta) =
    \frac{\partial \vect{D}_0}{\partial \E_0} =
    -\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial \E_0^2} =
    \frac{\varepsilon_r\varepsilon_0}{J}\F^{-T}\F^{-1} f_{EM}(\theta) \,.
\end{equation}

Considering that BDS experiments report the electric permittivity at undeformed configuration, expression \eqref{eqn:bds_epsilon_general} simplifies to
\begin{equation}
  \left.\varepsilon(\theta)\vphantom{a_{\substack{a\\a}}}\right|_{\substack{\F=\I\\ \E_0=\0}} =
    \varepsilon_r\varepsilon_0 f_{EM}(\theta) \,,
\end{equation}
which allows to easily calibrate the parameter $\varepsilon$ and the thermal coupling function $f_{EM}$. From the experimental data it can be observed little variation with respect to the temperature, hence, a smooth function such as the non-linear melting \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting} seems appropriate to capture the thermal variation of the electric permittivity.


\subsubsection{Step 6: Thermo-electro-mechanical validation}

Finally, a blind validation has been performed using the experimental data in \cite{Mehnert2021a} and the current model. In such situation, the measured stress will be a function of the stretch, voltage and temperature $P_{11}(\lambda,V,\theta)$ using the kinematics in \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_electro_mech}.



\subsection{Optimization problem}

The presented thermodynamic conjugates and simplified kinematics allow to construct an optimization problem which is independent of the step and the type of experiment. In this section the optimization methodology will be presented, as well as the statistical validation.


\subsubsection{Objective function}

The material parameters at each step were identified by minimizing the discrepancy between the experimental data and the theoretical response for the corresponding experiment types. The individual loss function $\mathcal{L}_k$ for an experiment $k$ is defined as the normalized mean squared error,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_k(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{N_k} \left(
    \frac{y_i^\text{pred}(\mathbf{p}) - y_i^\text{data}}{y_{\text{max},k}}
  \right)^2 ,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{p}$ is the set of parameters for the corresponding step and $N_k$ is the number of sampling points in the experiment $k$. The multi-objective total loss at each calibration step is composed by a set of experiments $\mathcal{S}$. The total loss can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\mathbf{p}) = \sum_{k\in\mathcal{S}} w_k \mathcal{L}_k(\mathbf{p}) \,,
\end{equation}
being $w_k$ the weight for each experiment, allowing to give higher priority to selected experiments. For instance, in a specific application, it could be interesting to prioritize the experiments closer to the operational stretch and operational temperature.



\subsubsection{Optimization algorithm}

To identify the material parameters while minimizing the risk of converging to local minima â€”a common challenge in non-linear thermo-electro-viscoelasticity- a two-stage hybrid optimization strategy is employed. The parameter vector $\mathbf{p}$ defined for each calibration step, is constrained within physically admissible lower ($\mathbf{l}_b$) and upper ($\mathbf{u}_b$) bounds. In the first stage, a global search is performed using the Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) algorithm, where the number of particles in the population and the number of iterations is adjusted according to the number of parameters and its nonlinearity. This heuristic stage ensures a broad exploration of the design space. Subsequently, the best candidate found by PSO is used as the initial seed for a local refinement stage using the Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm. This derivative-free local optimizer is utilized to achieve high-precision convergence to the final optimal set $\mathbf{p}^*$. The optimization workflow was implemented using the optimization library \cite{optimizationjl} of the Julia programming language, boosted with a parallel implementation \cite{utkarsh2024efficient}.


\subsubsection{Goodness of fit}

To quantitatively assess the predictive capability of the constitutive model once the optimal parameter set $\mathbf{p}^*$ is identified, the coefficient of determination, $R^2$, is computed for each experimental data set. This metric represents the proportion of the variance in the experimental observations that is captured by the model. For a given data set $k$, the $R^2$ value is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
R^2_k = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_k} (y_i^\text{data} - y_i^\text{pred}(\mathbf{p}^*))^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N_k} (y_i^\text{data} - \bar{y}_k^\text{\,data})^2}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{y}_k^\text{\,data}$ is the arithmetic mean of the experimental data for that specific test. An $R^2$ value approaching unity indicates that the model perfectly replicates the experimental results, while values significantly lower than 1 suggest regions where the physical assumptions or the parameter identification might require further refinement. This statistical measure is applied individually to calibration step.


\subsubsection{Uncertainty analysis}

To evaluate the reliability of the identified parameters, a local sensitivity and uncertainty analysis was performed. The covariance matrix was estimated from the inverse of the Hessian of the loss function at the optimum and has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{C} = \frac{\Phi(\mathbf{p}^*)}{n-p}\left(\nabla^2\Phi(\mathbf{p}^*)\right)^{-1} \,,
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of sampling points over the set of experiments and $p$ represents the number of parameters and the Hessian matrix was computed by finite differentiation.
Parameter uncertainties are reported as 95\% confidence intervals $CI_i$ based on the $t$-Student distribution with $n-p$ degrees of freedom as
\begin{equation}
  CI_i = p_i^* \pm t_{crit} \sqrt{C_{ii}} \,.
\end{equation}
Furthermore, a non-dimensional sensitivity index $S_i$ was computed to quantify the impact of each parameter on the objective function. The sensitivity index follows the definition
\begin{equation}
  S_i = \frac{p_i^{*^2}}{\Phi{p_i^*}}\frac{\partial \Phi{p_i^*}}{\partial p_i^*} \,.
\end{equation}
High $S_i$ values, over $10$, indicate well-posed identification, whereas values below $1$ highlight parameters that may by non-significative. Intermediate values indicate that those parameters are relevant but exhibit lower observability under the tested experimental conditions.
