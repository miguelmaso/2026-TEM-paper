
The constitutive model presented in section \ref{sec:constitutive equations} is general and can be applied to a wide variety of materials. In the context of EAPs, multiple authors have performed experimental testing, of the single physics, such as mechanical, thermal or electrical behaviour \cite{Hossain2012visco,Dippel2015} as well as the coupled behaviour of the combination of two \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020,Alkhoury2022} or the three physics \cite{Mehnert2021a,Sheng2012,Qin2023}.
The extensive experimental data for VHB polymer has motivated the choice of this material to validate the constitutive model, which involves not only fitting the best parameters, but the choice of specific mechanical models as well as thermodynamically-consistent coupling functions.

Beyond finding optimal parameters, the scope of the calibration is to validate the theoretical formulation and to show its capabilities. The proposed framework is general and the conclusions related to the methodology can be applied for other materials characterization.

\subsubsection{Data consistency}

One of the difficulties found when fitting optimal parameters is the lack of consistency on the data reported by different authors.
For instance, the same mechanical carried out at different laboratories under the same conditions of temperature, velocity and stretch, may report differences up to $50\%$. The same issue may happen with thermal as well as electrical properties.
There are several potential sources for the differences of the measured properties: VHB polymer is manufactured as an adhesive tape, hence, quality control focus on properties different from viscoelastic behaviour; VHB polymer is provided in different thicknesses, which can alter the curing process of the polymer; and finally, engineering tolerances are not defined for the current properties and can suffer variations from one batch of production to another one.

This observation would open a discussion about uncertainty of the material data and error propagation. Given the scope of this investigation is to assess the suitability of the numerical model to predict the behaviour of EAPs, only consistent data will be used for the characterization and is reported in table \ref{tab:calibration_steps}.



\subsection{Reduced constitutive equations for experimental configurations}

To ensure a unified optimization framework, each experimental test $k$ is treated as a discrete mapping between a vector of independent control variables $\vect{x}$ and a scalar observable response $y$. For each sampling point $i \in \{1, \dots, N_k\}$, the experimental data is represented as
%
\begin{equation}
  f_k : \; \vect{x}_i \to y_i
\end{equation}
%
where $\vect{x}_i$ denotes the local state (e.g., stretch $\lambda$, temperature $\theta$, time $t$, or voltage $V$) and $y_i$ represents the measured physical quantity, namely the specific heat capacity $c_v$, the longitudinal first Piola-Kirchhoff stress $P_{11}$, or the dielectric permittivity $\epsilon$. This scalar representation motivates the following two sections: first, the reduced kinematics necessary to define $\vect{x}$ according to the constraints of each test; and second, the derivation of the energy conjugate measures required to compute the model prediction $y_{pred}$ from the free energy density $\Psi$.



\subsubsection{Kinematics}

\paragraph{Thermal expansion and Jacobian}

The calorimetry tests as well as the thermo-mechanical tests are exposed to thermal variation inducing a volume change. In the absence of volumetric pressure, Eq. \eqref{eqn:volumetric_free_energy} yields the following relation for the Jacobian as a function of the temperature,
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:thermal_Jacobian}
    p = \frac{\partial\bar\Psi}{\partial J} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    J(\theta) = 1 + 3\alpha\frac{\theta_R}{\bar\gamma+1}
    \left(\left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\bar\gamma+1} - 1\right).
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Uniaxial stretch kinematics}

The deformation gradient of a mechanical test in the absence of thermal variation and in the absence of electric field is given by
\begin{equation}
  \F_M = \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & \lambda^{-1/2} & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & \lambda^{-1/2} \\
  \end{bmatrix} \,,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the stretch in the loading direction according to engineering notation. The deformation gradient is purely isochoric and the volumetric part is given by $J=1$.


\paragraph{Thermo-mechanical stretch kinematics}

In \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020,Alkhoury2022} the following protocol is presented:
\begin{displayquote}
  First, the specimens were placed inside the chamber and conditioned at the target temperature for fifteen minutes to achieve thermal homogeneity across the \si{0.5}{mm} thickness. Crucially, the specimens were mounted onto the grippers only after this thermal stabilization period. The protective silicon paper was then removed gently to ensure the test initiated without any pre-tension.  
\end{displayquote}
Consequently, the reference configuration for each experiment—corresponding to a stretch of $\lambda=1$ and zero nominal stress—is defined by the stress-free state of the material after free thermal expansion or contraction at the specific room temperature.
Accordingly, the deformation gradient tensor is straightforwardly found as the composition of volumetric and isochoric parts as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:F_thermo_mech}
  \F_{TM} = \bar\F \hat\F = J^{1/3} \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & \lambda^{-1/2} & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & \lambda^{-1/2} \\
  \end{bmatrix} \,,
\end{equation}
where $J$ is determined by Eq. \eqref{eqn:thermal_Jacobian}.


\paragraph{Thermo-electro-mechanical kinematics}

As stated before, thermo-electro-mechanical tests are not standardized and different loading protocols may be reported by authors, for instance, in \cite{Mehnert2021a},
%
\begin{displayquote}
  material samples with the same dimensions as for the electro-mechanical tests at reference temperature are cut and coated with carbon conductive grease.After the prepared sample is mounted onto the machine, the thermo chamber is closed and the target temperature is set. Once the thermo chamber is heated, the material is given fifteen minutes to fully heat up. After this wait time, the cyclic loading tests are conducted.
\end{displayquote}
%
Hence, the deformation gradient and electric potential field can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:F_thermo_electro_mech}
  \F_{TEM} = \begin{bmatrix}
    \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
    0 & J^{1/2}\lambda_2 & 0 \\
    0 & 0 & J^{1/2}(\lambda\lambda_2)^{-1} \\
  \end{bmatrix} , \quad
  \E = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ V/T_0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $T_0$ is the reference undeformed thickness and the electrical induced $\lambda_2$ must be such that the transverse stresses $P_{22}$ and $P_{33}$ equal to zero.
This condition will be enforced by the definition \eqref{eqn:experim_P11} in the following section.
It is important to remark that, unlike \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_mech}, \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_electro_mech} is related with non-zero axial stresses for $\lambda=1$ at temperatures different from $\theta_R$.



\subsubsection{Energy conjugate measures}


\paragraph{Uniaxial first Piola-Kirchhoff stress}

The evaluation of stresses with the aforementioned uniaxial kinematics and must be closed with appropriate boundary conditions (i.e., a traction-free state on the lateral surfaces of the specimen) by the addition of a hydrostatic pressure. The total Piola-Kirchhoff stress is given by $\P = \hat\P + pJ\F^{-T}$. Given that the lateral components must vanish ($P_{22} = P_{33} = 0$), the pressure $p$ is coupled to the constitutive response as:
\begin{equation}
  p = -\hat P_{22} J^{-1} F_{22}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the axial stress used in the objective function of the model calibration is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:experim_P11}
  P_{11} = \hat P_{11} + pJF_{11}^{-1} = \hat P_{11} - \hat P_{22} \frac{F_{22}}{F_{11}}
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Specific heat capacity}

The specific heat capacity can be directly computed from Eq. \eqref{eqn:specific_heat} with appropriate kinematics. For the specific case of DSC, the specimen is at an unstressed configuration $\P=\0$ and differentiation of \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} yields
\begin{equation}
  \label{eqn:calibration_cv}
  c_v(\theta) =
    -\theta \left. \frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial \theta^2} \right|_{\substack{\F=\bar\F\hfill\\ \E_0=\mathbf{0}}} =
    \left(c_v^0 + 3\alpha\kappa_R\bar\gamma(J-1)\right)
    \left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_R}\right)^{\bar\gamma} ,
\end{equation}
where the deviatoric and electric terms vanish. Note the presence of the bulk modulus in the expression, denoting a mechanical dependence of the specific heat, which is aligned with \cite{BonetGil2026}.


\paragraph{Dielectric constant}

Substitution of \eqref{eqn:full_free_energy_density_model} and \eqref{eqn:electro_mech_coupling_ref} into the definition of the dielectric permittivity yields
%
\begin{equation} \label{eqn:bds_epsilon_general}
  \varepsilon(\F,\E_0,\theta) =
    \frac{\partial \vect{D}_0}{\partial \E_0} =
    -\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial \E_0^2} =
    \frac{\varepsilon_r\varepsilon_0}{J}\F^{-T}\F^{-1} f_{\epsilon}(\theta) \,.
\end{equation}
%
Considering that BDS experiments report the electric permittivity at undeformed configuration, expression \eqref{eqn:bds_epsilon_general} simplifies to
\begin{equation}
  \left.\varepsilon(\theta)\vphantom{a_{\substack{a\\a}}}\right|_{\substack{\F=\I\hfill\\ \E_0=\0}} =
    \varepsilon_r\varepsilon_0 f_{\epsilon}(\theta) \,,
\end{equation}
allowing to easily calibrate the parameter $\varepsilon$ and the thermal coupling function $f_{\epsilon}$. From the experimental data it can be observed little variation with respect to the temperature, hence, a smooth function such as the non-linear melting \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting} seems appropriate to capture the thermal variation of the electric permittivity.



\subsection{Methodology: multi-step optimization strategy}
\label{sec:multi-step_optim}

The non-linearity and different components of the model, as well as the large number of parameters to be calibrated, justify, a staggered optimization strategy. The calibration process is divided into several steps, where each step focuses on a specific subset of parameters and a specific set of experiment. The results of each step are used as constant parameters for the next steps.

The previous definition of the kinematics in terms of a local state $\vect{x}_i$ and different measures $y_i$ will simplify the definition of an objective function in section \ref{sec:optim_problem}. Minimization of the error between the measured $y_i^\text{data}$ and the predicted $y_i^\text{pred}$ at the sampling points $\vect{x}_i^\text{data}$ will allow to identify the optimal parameters $\vect{p}$ minimizing the error. Table \ref{tab:calibration_steps} summarizes the staggered steps.

\paragraph{Step 1: Thermal properties} The reference temperature is arbitrarily set as $\theta_R=\SI{20}{\celsius}$ to make it coincide with the room temperature in \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020}. The thermal expansion coefficient is taken from the VHB data sheet \cite{3MVHB4910} ($\alpha=\num{1.8e-4}$) and the presence of the bulk modulus in \eqref{eqn:calibration_cv} allow indirect identification.

\paragraph{Step 2: Long-term model} The work presented in \cite{Liao_Mokarram2020} states that the stresses reported in the quasi-static one-cycle loading-unloading tests are close to the asymptotic stress of the creep tests at constant stretch. This observation allows to calibrate the hyperelastic response of the material from the quasi-static test, where the viscous effects are negligible. The hyperelastic response is calibrated by fitting the parameters of a non-linear Mooney-Rivlin model in Eq. \eqref{eqn:isochoric_mooney_rivlin}.

\paragraph{Step 3: Viscoelastic response} This step involves fitting the parameters $\mu_\alpha$ and $\tau_\alpha$ for each viscous branch $\alpha=1,\dots,n_\text{Maxw}$, where the number of branches $n$ is not known a priori.% The tangent parameter $\mu_\alpha$ corresponds to the neo-Hookean model in Eq. \eqref{eqn:isochoric_visco_neo_hokean}, and $\tau_n$ is the relaxation time.
Regarding the objective variables, a logarithmic transform has been applied to the relaxation times $\tau_\alpha$ to ensure the positivity of the parameters and to avoid the significant loss of the negative part of the distribution, since the relaxation times may be close to zero. The optimization is performed in the logarithmic space for $\tau_\alpha$ and in the linear space for $\mu_\alpha$. The number of branches $n$ is determined by analysing the sensitivity of the optimal parameters and the goodness of fit with respect to the number of branches.

\paragraph{Step 4: Thermo-mechanical coupling} In this step, the full set of experiments has been extended, leading to thermal variations and allowing to fit the thermal laws \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_melting} and \eqref{eqn:thermal_function_f_softening} for the hyperelastic response and the viscous response respectively. In section \ref{sec:experim_results} it will be discussed the sensitivity of the parameters, that justify using a single thermal function for all the viscous branches. A melting temperature $\theta_M=\SI{150}{\celsius}$ has been considered according to VHB technical specifications \cite{3MVHB4910}.

\paragraph{Step 5: Dielectric constant} The BDS experiments in \cite{Sheng2012} provide the measure of the dielectric permittivity $\varepsilon$ at a wide range of frequencies and different temperatures. A frequency of $\SI{1e1}{Hz}$ has been selected for the calibration, which is closer to the low-frequency experiments in \cite{Mehnert2021a}, allowing to focus on the thermo-mechanic coupling.

\paragraph{Step 6: Validation} Finally, a blind validation has been performed using the experimental data in \cite{Mehnert2021a} and the current model. In such situation, the measured stress will be a function of the stretch, voltage and temperature $P_{11}(\lambda,V,\theta)$ using the kinematics in \eqref{eqn:F_thermo_electro_mech}.



\subsection{Optimization problem}
\label{sec:optim_problem}

The presented states $\vect{x}$ and thermodynamic conjugates $y$ allow to construct an optimization problem which is independent of the step and the type of experiment. In this section the optimization methodology will be presented, as well as the statistical validation.


\subsubsection{Objective function}

The material parameters at each step were identified by minimizing the discrepancy between the experimental data and the theoretical response for the corresponding experiment types. The individual loss function $\mathcal{L}_k$ for an experiment $k$ is defined as the normalized mean squared error,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_k(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{i=1}^{N_k} \left(
    \frac{y_i^\text{pred}(\mathbf{p}) - y_i^\text{data}}{y_{\text{max},k}}
  \right)^2 ,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{p}$ is the set of parameters for the corresponding step and $N_k$ is the number of sampling points in the experiment $k$. The multi-objective total loss at each calibration step is composed by a set of experiments $\mathcal{S}$. The total loss can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\mathbf{p}) = \sum_{k\in\mathcal{S}} w_k \mathcal{L}_k(\mathbf{p}) \,,
\end{equation}
being $w_k$ the weight for each experiment, allowing to give higher priority to selected experiments. For instance, in a specific application, it could be interesting to prioritize the experiments closer to the operational stretch and operational temperature.



\subsubsection{Optimization algorithm}

To identify the material parameters while minimizing the risk of converging to local minima —a common challenge in non-linear thermo-electro-viscoelasticity- a two-stage hybrid optimization strategy is employed. The parameter vector $\mathbf{p}$ defined for each calibration step, is constrained within physically admissible lower ($\mathbf{l}_b$) and upper ($\mathbf{u}_b$) bounds. In the first stage, a global search is performed using the Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) algorithm, where the number of particles in the population and the number of iterations is adjusted according to the number of parameters and its nonlinearity. This heuristic stage ensures a broad exploration of the design space. Subsequently, the best candidate found by PSO is used as the initial seed for a local refinement stage using the Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm. This derivative-free local optimizer is utilized to achieve high-precision convergence to the final optimal set $\mathbf{p}^*$. The optimization workflow was implemented using the optimization library \cite{optimizationjl} of the Julia programming language, boosted with a parallel implementation \cite{utkarsh2024efficient}.


\subsubsection{Goodness of fit}

To quantitatively assess the predictive capability of the constitutive model once the optimal parameter set $\mathbf{p}^*$ is identified, the coefficient of determination, $R^2$, is computed for each experimental data set. This metric represents the proportion of the variance in the experimental observations that is captured by the model. For a given data set $k$, the $R^2$ value is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
R^2_k = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_k} (y_i^\text{data} - y_i^\text{pred}(\mathbf{p}^*))^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N_k} (y_i^\text{data} - \bar{y}_k^\text{\,data})^2}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{y}_k^\text{\,data}$ is the arithmetic mean of the experimental data for that specific test. An $R^2$ value approaching unity indicates that the model perfectly replicates the experimental results, while values significantly lower than 1 suggest regions where the physical assumptions or the parameter identification might require further refinement. This statistical measure is applied individually to calibration step.


\subsubsection{Uncertainty analysis}

To evaluate the reliability of the identified parameters, a local sensitivity and uncertainty analysis was performed. The covariance matrix was estimated from the inverse of the Hessian of the loss function at the optimum and has the form
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{C} = \frac{\Phi(\mathbf{p}^*)}{n-p}\left(\nabla^2\Phi(\mathbf{p}^*)\right)^{-1} \,,
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the total number of sampling points over the set of experiments and $p$ represents the number of parameters and the Hessian matrix was computed by finite differentiation.
Parameter uncertainties are reported as 95\% confidence intervals $CI_i$ based on the $t$-Student distribution with $n-p$ degrees of freedom as
\begin{equation}
  CI_i = p_i^* \pm t_{crit} \sqrt{C_{ii}} \,.
\end{equation}
Furthermore, a non-dimensional sensitivity index $S_i$ was computed to quantify the impact of each parameter on the objective function. The sensitivity index follows the definition
\begin{equation}
  S_i = \frac{p_i^{*^2}}{\Phi{p_i^*}}\frac{\partial \Phi{p_i^*}}{\partial p_i^*} \,.
\end{equation}
High $S_i$ values, over $10$, indicate well-posed identification, whereas values below $1$ highlight parameters that may by non-significative. Intermediate values indicate that those parameters are relevant but exhibit lower observability under the tested experimental conditions.
